Parents of Eden Westbrook found dead in Tasmanian park pushing for new inquest

Publish date: 2024-07-17

The devastated parents of a teenage girl who was found dead in a small town on the Tasmanian coast are adamant she did not take her own life and are still fighting for answers eight years on. 

Eden Westbrook, 15, was found in Fisherman's Memorial Park in St Helens in the state's north-east, two hours from Launceston, at dawn in February 18, 2015. 

Despite police quickly arriving and awkwardly attempting to shield the scene from the view of the public, Ms Westbrook's body was seen by multiple people, including a bus full of schoolchildren, due to her position in a tree.

Word spread through the town of about 2,200 people and reached Eden's parents, Jason and Amanda Westbrook, who raced to the park to be confronted by their little girl on public display, with police not wanting to disturb the scene. 

Eden had stormed off after an argument about her mobile phone the previous evening and they had spent much of the night scouring the streets for her.

Eden's parents, Jason and Amanda Westbrook, were confronted with the haunting scene of their 15-year-old daughter Fisherman's Memorial Park

Coroner Olivia McTaggart ruled in September, 2016 that Eden had died by suicide. 

An inquest was not held with the coroner relying solely on the information from the police investigation. 

Her parents firmly believe she did not take her own life and are critical of the investigation, saying vital information had been overlooked by police.

They also say more information has emerged in the years since, and claim a psychologist contacted them last year saying Eden and another underage girl were being sexually groomed by a powerful man.

Tasmanian newspaper The Mercury has been consistently following the case and earlier this year published sensational claims an anonymous man had visited the Westbrooks' at their home and told them he knew what happened to Eden. 

The couple had been in their garden after moving from NSW to St Helen's a few years earlier to run a landscaping business, when the man drove up their driveway.

He told them that he had been drinking with a friend and they had got talking on the topic of Eden.

The man told them the teenager did not take her own life but that she had been to a party, been given an overdose, and the way her body was found had been staged. 

Mr Westbrook call his friend, Sydney based lawyer Peter Lavac, who flew down to meet them and recorded the man's claims on audio.

Eden Westbrook died in February 2015. Her death was deemed as suicide but her father Jason believes it may have been foul play

Eden's parents are pushing for a new inquiry which they hope will help put her memory to rest and heal a wound still lingering beneath the surface of the remote Tasmanian community

On the recording heard by the newspaper the man, who is well known in the fishing community, said he had been with someone a few weeks beforehand in Launceston.

'(My friend) heard there was more to the story and supposedly there were two people involved in putting her up that tree … a man and a woman.'

'They'd used a rope from off one of the boats … down at the wharf.

'(Someone who knew) the man who'd supposedly put Eden up the tree had passed this information on to the person I was talking to.'

The visitor provided the names of a man and much younger woman, who are known in the town but have never been named by media. 

He explained the man had allegedly confessed his involvement to his then girlfriend, who in turn told her mother who had told the visitor's friend. 

The Westbrooks' said the younger girl the visitor identified had been known to be acting increasingly bizarrely in the years since Eden died and had on occasions gone up to members of their family ranting that she was 'sorry'. 

Mr Lavac spoke to a Tasmania Police inspector and offered to let police interview the visitor however, when he refused to let a lawyer be present, he rescinded the offer, reports The Australian. 

Mr Westbrook told the newspaper this week he believes part of the reason he has struggled in his push for further investigation into Eden's case is because he is considered an outsider.

A memorial at the foreshore park where Eden was found in St Helens, Tasmania

The pair, who have seven children and are originally from the Sunshine Coast, moved to the town in the early 2000s but are still not considered locals, according to Mr Westbrook. 

'We have just been sustaining an attack on our character as mainlanders that have come to Tasmania and are troublemakers,' he told the publication. 

'So we've sort of been discredited with any attempts we've made to get to the truth.'

The coroner in her findings said Eden was a caring person who was doing well in Year 10 and was highly regarded by both her students and teachers.

But she noted her school internet search history showed, perhaps not uncommonly for a teenager, searches on topics such as unprotected sex, depression and drugs.  

She deemed Eden had been depressed, had previously attempted to harm herself, and had written a note six months earlier indicating an 'intent to end her life'. 

Mr Westbrook strongly denies that this was the case saying 'at no stage did Eden try to take her own life'.

Despite a large gap between the teenager leaving her parents house and arriving at the park remaining unaccounted for, the coroner said she was 'satisfied there are no suspicious circumstances surrounding Eden's death or that any other person was involved'.

The Westbrooks are pushing for a new open and transparent inquest to be held that would examine new information and interview new witnesses.

They hope this would not only get them answers, but help put Eden's memory to rest and heal a wound still lingering beneath the surface of the remote Tasmanian community.

Police told The Australian: 'Subsequent allegations have been investigated and there is no evidence to substantiate them.

'There is no evidence to suggest Miss Westbrook was groomed by a person in a position of authority. 

'Police have made multiple attempts to further engage with Miss Westbrook's family. We recognise the lasting emotional toll that remains with them and the broader community.'
