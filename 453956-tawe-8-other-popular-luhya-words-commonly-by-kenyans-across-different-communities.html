Tawe, 8 Other Popular Luhya Words Commonly used by Kenyans Across Different Communities

Publish date: 2024-10-06

Whereas most Kenyans love to communicate in their own dialects, the unity in diversity has made it possible for people from other tribes to embrace different aspects from across the country.

One of such cases is language, and the Luhyia community seems to be doing well at contributing words and phrases that offer a national appeal.

It is against this backdrop that TUKO.co.ke shares some of the common Luhyia words that are currently used and understood by all tribes in Kenya.

Tawe

Tawe in the Luhya dialect means No.

The word started gaining publicity through football as rivals Gor Mahia and AFC Leopards threw banter over the fielding of players.

Between 2009 and 2012 when both teams were at the top of their game, it was common to see fans on social media complaining when a player was included in the starting eleven.

It has since become popular in political circles when individuals are talking about their choice of candidates and those they cannot vote for.

An example is "Mukhisa tawe," a phrase that was thrown around quite oftenly when the politician declared his intention to vie for presidency.

Vaane

Vaane is a word used across all Luhya subtribes to denote sympathy. It can be used to express the gravity of a situation that has gone wrong.

In a literal sentence, vaane would be an equivalent of the word surely, for instance;

"Vaane (Surely!) Did you have to chase him away at night?"

Omwami

The word Omwami was originally meant to be a title that was used in honor of someone respectful, mostly village chiefs.

Over the years, the title has been stripped down and is now used to refer to a fellow man during light Swahili conversations for instance;

"Omwami, leo umeamua haufanyi kazi?" (Man, you've decided you won't work today?)

Senje

Senje is a title that is used by an individual when referring to his or her aunt, but these days the meaning has been changed to just any woman.

In one of Sauti Sol's tracks, they allude to a Senje to whom the song is dedicated to, one who is nothing near an in-law.

The lyrics imply that the woman in the song is just a pretty damsel that they fell in love with and hope that she will get to know about it.

Mulamwa

Mulamwa means in-law. However, just like Senje, the word has over time become a common reference to anyone within one's circles.

It is against this backdrop that one of Kenya's comedians picked it as his stage name, one that has stuck so well that many of his fans no longer remember his real name.

Khandi

Khandi simply means "again" in Luhyia land, but it is the manner in which the native speakers used it that gave it a national appeal.

This happened when Luhyias started adding it to every sentence as a way of stressing a point.

It has over time become a national word that is used and understood by all communities.

Maayi

Maayi is a Bukusu word for mum or a woman. However, it's popularly used among women and young girls as an affectionate pet name the same way ladies would refer to each other as mummy or just mum.

Ingokho

Chicken is a common delicacy all across Kenya, with each tribe having a different name for the bird.

Luos call in gweno, Kikuyus refer to it as ngoko, however, it is the Luhyia way of calling it that has since been embraced by all.

It is due to this that folks looking to buy chicken, even those who are non-Luhyias, are quick to use the term "ingokho."

Mundu Khu Mundu

Mundu khu mundu was initially a word used in battles to mean that two individuals would face off man to man.

Interestingly, it has since lost the war-front meaning and is these days used to simply means that people will meet face to face.
