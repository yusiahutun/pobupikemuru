Scripps heiress Tori Scripps Carmody arrested again after stealing adoptive mom's car

Publish date: 2024-04-12

Jerry Oppenheimer is a New York Times bestselling author who has written biographies of American icons such as the Kennedys, Clintons, Hiltons and the Johnson & Johnson Band-Aid family

Three years after poor little rich girl Victoria 'Tori' Scripps Carmody was released from prison after promising a judge that she'd quit drugs and get on the straight and narrow, she's back in trouble with the law, DailyMail.com can exclusively report.

Scripps media heiress Tori, who turned 27 in June, was arrested by Vermont State Police and the South Burlington Police Department in April, for allegedly stealing her adoptive mother's sports car and pawning some of the Scripps family's valuable heirloom jewelry.

Tori was charged with operating the 2016 Audi TT sports car 'without the owner's consent,' and possession of stolen property - diamond and pearl earrings belonging to her aunt and adoptive mother Mary Scripps.

The earrings, which police say Tori pawned in a shop in South Burlington had been passed down to Mary by Tori's grandmother.

At a hearing in the Chittenden Criminal Division of Vermont Superior Court on May 22, Tori was released without bail on a set of strict conditions, banning the one-time heroin addict from buying or using 'regulated drugs without a valid prescription.'

She was also ordered not to 'abuse or harass' Mary Scripps or her husband, Robert Carmody, or 'go into or onto' their property unless they were present.

Listed as a 'transient,' the pretty heiress was ordered to give the court clerk or her attorney an address and phone number where she could always be reached. 

 Victoria 'Tori' Scripps Carmody (pictured right in her 2015 mugshot) was arrested again in April after allegedly stealing her mother's car in South Burlington, Vermont

Tori, is the daughter of socialite and heiress Anne Scripps Douglas (pictured with her two other daughters Alex and Annie) a descendant of famed newspaper publisher E.W. Scripps. Tori ) was three years old when she witnessed her mother's grisly murder in 1993

She was also given three days to report to the Williston headquarters of the Vermont State Police to have her fingerprints and mug shot taken.

Tori can face jail time or new charges if she violates her release conditions.  

On Wednesday August 1, her attorney met with Chittenden Criminal Division court staff to discuss the latest in a long history of criminal cases involving Tori, and another hearing was scheduled for August 22.

Tori's arrest comes three years after she was released from prison after serving a four-month sentence for violating her probation following her drug conviction in 2011.

She admitted to using opiates and associating with drug dealers but was let off the hook after writing a 10-page letter to the judge with plans to rehabilitate herself.

Tori's troubles with the law are believed to be influenced by her tragic past after she witnessed her father murder her mother at three years old.

Anne Scripps, a 47-year-old socialite was bludgeoned on New Year's Eve 1993, in a bedroom of her mansion in Bronxville, a New York City suburb.

The heiress was attacked with a claw hammer and beaten on the face and head by Scott Douglas, her abusive second husband, an alcoholic house painter.

When police arrived at the Scripps mansion, they discovered a frightened Tori near the blood-spattered body of her mother.

The distraught child told homicide detectives, 'Daddy gave mommy boo-boos. Why is mommy wearing war paint?' 

The traumatized toddler was describing the horrific spatter of blood she saw on her mother's near-lifeless body. Anne Scripps remained in a coma for six days before she died.

One of Tori's half-sisters said, 'Scott took a hammer to my mother's head and a steak knife to her face. The intrusions in her head were so big they thought they were gunshot wounds. Imagine finding your mother like that, her head torn apart and her face all ripped up.'

Anne Scripps was 47 when she was attacked with a claw hammer and beaten in the face and head by Scott Douglas (right) her abusive second husband, an alcoholic house painter. Douglas committed suicide by jumping off of the Tappan Zee Bridge hours later

Tori was adopted and raised by her Aunt Mary Scripps and her husband Robert Carmody (pictured) in Vermont after her parents' death. She was ordered to not 'abuse and harass' her parents as part of her release conditions 

A couple of hours later, Scott Douglas, who was a decade younger than his wife, committed suicide by jumping off of the Tappan Zee Bridge that spanned the Hudson River between Westchester County, N.Y., and New Jersey. 

Weeks later his body was found on the Bronx side of the river, ending a nationwide search for him and giving some closure to the devastated Scripps family,

Anne's great-great grandfather was James Edmund Scripps who founded the influential and powerful Scripps-owned Detroit News in 1873, and established the Evening News Association, which was sold to the Gannett Company in 1985 for $717 million.

And she was the great-great-great granddaughter of the larger-than-life, eccentric founder of the entire Scripps's media empire, Edward Willis 'E.W.' Scripps, who had a history as an alcoholic, philanderer and morphine addict. 

Today, the Scripps company owns TV stations, small and big-city newspapers, news and feature syndicates, and brought to the world such notable funny page characters as Peanuts and L'il Abner. Even the National Spelling Bee was started by the Scripps enterprises in 1925.

The horrors of the Scripps family took on an even more frightening and bizarre twist some 15 years after the murder of Anne Scripps when one of Tori's two half-sisters -- Annie Scripps Morell, a divorcee with a teenage son -- committed suicide from the exact same point on the bridge from where her stepfather leaped to his death.

The horrific images of seeing her mother murdered and the suicide of her close half-sister would remain with Tori Scripps for the rest of her life, destroying her emotionally and physically, and leading her into a life of crime, drug addiction, and incarceration, according to family members and friends.

After a court battle over orphaned Tori's custody, she was adopted by Mary Gibbs Scripps, her deceased mother's sister who was a Scripps heiress, a former Ford model, and an aspiring actress, who raised her in rural Vermont.

Party girl: After her mother's death, Tori (pictured left with a friend) was said to have rebelled and blew through her inherited Scripps millions in just a few years, allegedly using drugs to bury the pain she felt from the loss of her mother

But Tori rebelled, according to sources. She blew through her inherited Scripps millions in just a few years, allegedly using drugs to bury the pain she felt from the loss of her mother. 

The Scripps family had actually gone to court in a desperate, failed attempt to keep her from her inheritance. 

It was claimed that Tori, then 18, was incapable of handling her seven-figure fortune let alone her five-figure monthly allowance.

Tori was first arrested in August 2011 when Vermont State Police stopped her BMW. 

She and the two men with whom she was then living in Burlington were arrested and convicted of having in their possession 193 bags of heroin, and of using her apartment as a drug den to distribute heroin and crack cocaine.   

In February 2012, she pleaded guilty in U.S. District Court to running a crack house in Burlington, Vermont, near where she was raised.

She was incarcerated for about a month, was permitted to go into rehab in Florida, but was thrown out of a halfway house in the Sunshine State for violations and returned to jail. A plea deal was made, and she went into drug treatment. 

The alleged victim of her most recent run-in with authorities was her adoptive mother, Mary Scripps, 66, according to police.

Exclusively reported by DailyMail.com in April 2015, Tori had penned a heart-rending 10-page letter, telling U.S. District Court Judge William K. Sessions III that she was giving up a life of drugs and the criminal acts that came with her addictions. 

She wrote the letter after her arrest in November 2014 on charges of violating her probation by using drugs, possessing drug paraphernalia and associating with 'persons engaged in drug using and distribution.'

'Throughout my addiction I have suffered so many consequences,' she wrote. 'Every time I think things cannot possibly get any worse, they do.

'As a result of my addiction I have lost family, friends, hope for my future, my own values and beliefs, self-esteem, pets, educational opportunities, respect from others and for myself, my freedom, and the list goes on…'

In her letter, she headed one of the pages, 'My Plan For Success.'

She said she planned on taking '2-3 classes' at a community college, and to get a daily dose of Suboxone – a high-risk drug used to treat opioid addiction - at a Vermont clinic. She also promised to go to session at Turning Point 'and make (sic) some sober support.'

A federal prosecutor who supervised Tori's case in 2014-2015 told DailyMail.com: 'We have some serious opiate problems in Vermont right now. For many people beating that kind of addiction takes multiple attempts…These addicts facilitate the drug business at some level. They are just not customers, but are helping the drug trade work, and that's bad for the community.'

One of her attorneys claimed in the past that along with Tori's addiction, she suffered from Post Traumatic Stress Disorder and abandonment.

In 1997, when Tori was in junior high school, she saw the re-airing of a mostly fictionalized re-creation made-for-TV movie on the Lifetime cable network about her mother's murder that caused her more emotional stress, especially when classmates taunted her about how her mother died and being adopted. 

Mary Scripps and her husband were said to believe that the anxiety from the film and the aftermath caused Tori to perform badly in school, and possibly turning to drugs.

In her letter to the judge, she pledged to move back in with her adoptive parents.

But, with the latest charges against Tori, that relationship doesn't appear to have worked out.

As a result of her promises, she was released in 2015 from the 1,817-inmate Metropolitan Detention Center, in Brooklyn, New York, where she had been serving out the remainder of her time, her last known incarceration.

'Tori was like a princess among robbers, murderers and rapists,' said a source familiar with her incarceration there. 'It was sad, but she did the crime and she did the time.'

Now, with her latest alleged crime, she could face possible jail time again.
