Vestiaire Collectives Archive Room has landed

Publish date: 2024-06-23

When you think of iconic fashion moments, what springs to mind? Perhaps it's Madonna opening her Blond Ambition tour in a conical Jean Paul Gaultier bustier, or a young Naomi Campbell taking a runway tumble in Vivienne Westwood’s purple crocodile platforms.

Some fashion creations are so memorable, any avid fashion fan will be able to recall their story: and this month, Vestiaire Collective is celebrating those history-steeped threads. Cementing its reputation as the home of inimitable vintage pieces, the resale platform has resurrected its Archive Room: an ongoing project that celebrates fashion history and the designs that have defined the decades.

The Archive Room will host themed online curations, allowing shoppers to purchase some of the most precious pieces from runways past, with prices ranging from the accessible (a £40 Versace belt) to the wholly exclusive (a £44,000 Hermès Kelly). A fresh edit will arrive every month, each celebrating a different facet of fashion: highlights include John Galliano’s dreamlike craftsmanship for Dior, and the most impactful designs from the mind of Miuccia Prada.

Rose Hartman//Getty Images

Following the smaller, swiftly sold-out archive collection that launched last year, the project returns today with Icons Only: a curated selection of the most famous vintage pieces in the world. These are designs any runway twitcher will recognise in an instant, sourced from across the globe and gathered neatly in one place, ready to be rehomed.

A slick black leather bondage top will be remembered from Gianni Versace’s sexually charged AW92 Miss S&M collection: a controversial design that A-listers clamoured to get their hands on. The Hermès Quelle Idole (say it at speed to get the joke) was created in a limited run to commemorate the Millennium – now, these quirky Kellies are more sought-after than ever, and often the main event at handbag auctions.

Vivienne Westwood’s iconic 12-inch platforms may not be designed for walking in, but they’ll look fabulous in a display case. Similarly, Gucci’s provocative silver handcuff bracelets (only 25 of which were produced) were designed by Tom Ford in 1998, and ostentatiously hung in the store’s windows once Patrizia Reggiani was convicted for plotting the fateful end of her ex-husband Maurizio Gucci.

For the Alexander McQueen collectors, there’s an especially sentimental find in store: a one-shouldered, duchesse-satin dress from the late designer’s final AW10 collection, inspired by the angels of Byzantine art. The duchesse-satin design features a digitally rendered depiction of Jean Fouquet’s 1445 work, Virgin and Child. Unfinished at the time of his death, McQueen’s close-knit design team completed his vision and presented it in a Paris ballroom, part runway and part eulogy.

The arrival of the Archive Room is well timed: the vintage market is set to boom this year. Research has predicted the second-hand fashion market to be worth billions more than fast fashion by 2028, and Vestiaire Collective finds vintage sells 30% faster than non-vintage listings, with supply from sellers up 22% year-on-year.

getty images

"Vintage is one of the fastest growing categories on Vestiaire Collective, and in the past five years it’s been at the heart of what our community is searching to buy," says Sophie Hersan, co-founder and fashion director of Vestiaire Collective. "While it was once associated only with fashion collectors and connoisseurs, we’re seeing a greater appreciation for vintage across the board because each piece has a unique history, an emotion and a story to tell, as well as being the more sustainable alternative, making it extremely valuable."

Just look to the recent red carpet season, where best-dressed lists were peppered with celebrities in dresses from decades past. Margot Robbie wore a crystal-soaked corset dress from Vivienne Westwood’s SS98 collection to the AACTA awards in February, while the Grammys provided a visual feast for vintage magpies, with Olivia Rodrigo in classic Nineties Versace and Miley Cyrus in show-stopping archival Bob Mackie. But surely the vintage moment of the year so-far has to be Zendaya in a full vintage Mugler armour at the premiere of Dune 2.

Jeff Spicer//Getty Images

Of course, sustainability issues are a key driver here – the fashion industry is the third highest contributor to global pollution levels – but what Vestiaire Collective does better than most is to frame second-hand shopping as not just an eco-friendly choice, but a wholly luxurious one. You won’t find a single dusty cast-off on the retailer’s hand-vetted selection, but rather a treasure trove of runway relics that’ll make so much more of a statement than a fresh-off-the-shelves new-season piece.

The platform is laser-focused on reframing the vintage shopping experience this year, having recently introduced more stringent criteria for pieces listed on the site, removing items that don’t meet the age or condition standards. This attention to detail has clearly nurtured real trust from fashion’s most invested collectors: recent sales facilitated by the site include the tropical Versace dress worn by Jenifer Lopez to the Grammys in 2000 and a rare Hermès Himalaya Kelly in matte crocodile leather and palladium, which fetched €130,000.

The Archive Room’s supporting campaign, which sees famous portrait subjects dressed in the likes of Versace and Dior, is a tongue in cheek comment on the much-debated relationship between art and fashion: and surely, when you consider pieces with the cultural impact of Versace’s bondage leatherwork, or Vivienne Westwood’s punk platforms, the connection is clear to see.
