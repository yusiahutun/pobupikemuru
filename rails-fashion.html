How Rails fashion became a global success

Publish date: 2024-02-29

It takes a strong fashion brand to have survived the tests of Covid, let alone to have seen significant growth, but such is the situation Rails finds itself in. The go-to accessible label for off-duty Californian style (Cindy Crawford, Kendall Jenner and Emily Ratajkowski are all fans), Rails has gone from small start-up to a fully fledged empire sold in more than 40 countries around the world - including two new stores in London in the desirable locations of Covent Garden and Westbourne Grove.

While other fashion companies were forced to close as a result of the pandemic, Rails benefitted from shoppers’ newfound interest in comfortable clothing - its easy loungewear, sleep sets and jumpers felt even more appealing as we worked from home. And comfort is key to the brand; so important, in fact, that it develops its own fabrics to create almost cashmere-like softness. “The ‘hand-feel’ of our brand is such a major component in why we’ve been successful,” says founder and LA native Jeff Abrams. “People being in a store to feel our fabrics was super important as we evolved. [Covid] turned out to be a good time to invest in retail as a lot of people were closing their doors, and we were one of the few people going to landlords saying we want to buy a space.”

Our product makes people feel something special

If the brand was a much-loved insider secret before Covid, there has been a groundswell since. The growth of Rails in the past year alone is impressive - its e-commerce business has increased by 200 per cent, while its wholesale avenue is up by 125 per cent. Popularity is so high for certain styles, including its flattering Syd LBD and daisy-covered Perci jumper, that they have to be regularly restocked. Its mail-order catalogue, made using recycled paper, reaches more than one million customers every season. “We’re making classic styles that customers can wear season after season,” he says. “It’s not totally fashion-driven, we’re not fast fashion. Our product makes people feel something special.”

Rails has tapped into a broad customer demographic - you’re as likely to see a 50+ woman in one of the timeless shirts or versatile white midi dresses, as much as you are a 20-something in one of its floral, flippy minis. “There’s very much a mother-daughter customer buying our brand,” says Abrams. “If I was thinking about a celebrity family who epitomises our world, it would be the Crawfords: Cindy, Randi, Kaia and Presley. We’re consistent; we offer pieces that are true to the heritage of the brand, but we also deliver newness so there’s a reason to come back.”

It helps when women like Gisele and Kendall Jenner wear our shirts

Abrams first launched his label in 2008 after an inter-railing trip round Europe gave him the idea of fusing breezy Californian style with European elegance. Without any technical background in fashion, he started small with caps and hoodies, touting his wares as a door-to-door salesman. Before long, he created what would be Rails’ signature - the Hunter shirt in cotton plaid. It was a great style, but without a real point of difference, which led him to consider the material more deeply. “I had this scarf that was super drapey in a Tencel-blend, so I went to the factory that was making our shirts and said I wanted to make our shirt in this particular fabric,” he recalls. “We went through a bunch of iterations that would be durable enough to last as a shirt, but soft enough to be elegant in a casual way. Once I had that, we spent every season doing fabric innovation in a lot of categories.”

The next big milestone came in 2015 when supermodel Gisele wore a Rails shirt to the Super Bowl to watch her husband Tom Brady play. The image of her wearing it was plastered over every television screen in the US and beyond, and within seconds, the style sold out. “We weren’t really direct-to-consumer at the point, so we had a website but it wasn’t optimised to handle that amount of people,” laughs Abrams. “Certainly, it helps when women like Gisele and Kendall Jenner wear our shirts.”

As we move beyond the pandemic, the Rails woman has migrated to versatile dresses, slouchy tailoring and also to denim, the label’s latest new category. A new eco collection, made using organic cotton and recycled cotton, is also proving popular. “In the garment business, it’s hard to be 100 per cent sustainable,” Abrams admits. “We’re trying to make steps every season to improve what we’re doing. Customers want more access to what you’re making, but also to who you are and what you stand for.”

In a challenging time for retail, Rails proves that a great product that speaks to what the shopper wants and needs will get you everywhere. “When you first start a business, everyone always wants to tell you no - they tell you someone else is doing that, or we already have a similar line that we carry,” says Abrams. “Perseverance is critical to anyone starting a business: you have to fight through all of the nos to eventually get a little momentum that will carry you forward.”
